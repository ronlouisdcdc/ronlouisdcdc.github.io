A Concept Paper on the Idea of a Beautiful Woman as Perceived by Media

     Is there an exact definition of beauty? Or does it only rely on the culture or ‘taste’ the observer possesses? The answer depends on the circumstances. Still, through the eyes of the media, beauty follows a certain image; an established standard which is considered orthodox.  

     To further discern a precise definition of beauty, according to the Merriam-Webster dictionary, beauty means “the quality or aggregate of qualities in a person or thing that gives pleasure to the senses or pleasurably exalts the mind or spirit: loveliness”.

     But what is media and how did it generated a conformity about how people see beauty? Well, as stated in the Oxford dictionary, media is “the main means of mass communication (broadcasting, publishing, and the internet) regarded collectively”.

     As time passed by, the world spawned change. From innovative infrastructures to convenient conveyance to macro communication, the world emanated change. However, something has been kept constantly worsening through the progression of media – stereotypes, more particularly, the stereotype on how media perceives a beautiful woman.

     Nowadays, supposed “millennials” are critically impelled by media, let alone the social medias. Who are millennials, by the way? Millennials are those individuals who reached adulthood around the 21st century. They are the ones who are developed, or are still being continuously developed, in an electronics-polluted era. Therefore, in just a click away, they could send or see any news immediately.

     Hence, media gravely affects a person’s perception of the world; it sets a paradigm for everybody to follow. Specifically speaking, it sets a beauty criterion which is universally followed by hundreds of millions. Among all the splendor of renowned and fabulous actors, actresses and singers circulating the net, the definition of ‘beautiful’ arose – sexy and perfect.

     One can plainly witness that for their social media post to gain positive comments, one must follow the ways of their idols; as if it is an unspoken decree. Something uncommon or atypical receives the opposite. Thus, the image of a beautiful woman has been universally set; a white, pretty, unblemished, thin and sexy woman is a woman commendable to be in media.

     The journalist strongly believes that the connotation commenced when the media was used as a medium for advertising. The style of marketing undoubtedly screams the concept that the ideal female is white, thin, sexily clothed, and above all, docile. The majority of companies entice the general public into purchasing their produces by emphasizing one’s charm and flawlessness. The television advertisement films use well-known stars to endorse a number of products. For example, skincare line, Pond’s, has current shining stars of the Philippines showbiz such as Kathryn Bernardo, Julia Barretto, Nadine Lustre, and Toni Gonzaga as its ambassadors to pursuit the product’s tagline of timeless beauty by assuring the viewers that they can be as beautiful as these actresses given that they prioritize beauty care in their lives. Well in fact, these specific actresses have had underwent plenty of artificial beautifying sessions. Regardless of that, provoking or lewd images of women’s somewhat clothed or half-naked bodies are notably widespread in magazines to lure more consumers and to make covers more conspicuous – as they thought so.

     In a research entitled Anorexia and Models, the researcher stated that “The way the media portrays women provides a false image of the ‘ideal’ female body type that causes teenage girls to feel alienated in their own body.” Through the viewpoint of the mass media, thinness is idealized, and it is demanded for women to be deemed "attractive." Imageries in advertisements, television, and music usually portray the "ideal woman" as tall, fair skinned, and skinny with a tubular body and blonde hair.

     Furthermore, media also promotes false beauty as more and more people attempt to fulfill the standards set. A beautiful woman, whether viewed in a commercial, television show, newspaper or a Facebook post, has loads of make-up applied on her face. According to media, one must conceal her flaws so as to be eligible as a perfect gorgeous woman. Thus, it can be denoted that these standards urge women to be untrue of themselves for the sake of acquiring the confidence of being standard in the society.

     The issue does not close here. In terms of the body that a woman of media should exhibit, the sexiness and voluptuousness is important; a beautiful woman must boast a magnificent figure – cinched waists and round chests and bottoms. Accordingly, nearly all women considered beautiful are far beneath the healthy weight for their body, and do not frequently eat adequately to sustain their health. A great detriment of eating an apple for a day must be performed to attain the ridiculous body shape.

     Consequently, in conforming with the absolute image of a beautiful woman as shown by media, ladies lean towards the idea of modifying their entirety. The obsession and delusion of becoming absolutely beautiful leads to the artificial beautification, of all kinds, may it be as minor as Botox or as major as whole face transformation. Conversely, this does not cost only a penny, but a huge amount of money must be compensated. Although, because the desire to embody the “dream girl”, ladies willingly take the bait of undergoing cosmetic surgeries in the hands of amateur surgeons for a cheaper price. Thus, the rise of plastic surgery flops, causing the aspiring young adults bawling.

     Henceforth, there can be no doubt that through the eyes of media, a beautiful woman is a perfect woman – with an attractive face, banging figure, and flawless milky-white skin. But what can be seen in media must remain an image only, not a dictator.

     As the sun finally settles and the vast darkness unfolds, one thing remains; media does not define a woman’s worth and genuineness. Women must bear in mind that the judgement of others must not, and would definitely not, dictate how they want to live their lives. It is only themselves that could be the captain of their own ships of fate. As the famed poet, William Ernest Henley, once quoted:

     “I am the master of my fate, I am the captain of my soul.”
