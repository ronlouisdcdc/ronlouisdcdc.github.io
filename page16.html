Goyo: Ang Batang Heneral: A Movie Review
     Jerrold Tarog’s historical film, Goyo: Ang Batang Heneral struck a chord not only in the blockbusters with its successful commercial run, but also in the heart of Filipinos.
General Gregorio Del Pilar, portrayed by Paulo Avelino, was depicted in the movie as the “Batang Heneral” with the moniker of Goyo. Goyo led the revolutionary army, together with his comrades, who marched all the way to Dagupan in one of their expeditions, and, during this certain trip, he met his soon-to-be-lover. For historical contexture, Goyo was openly portrayed as a flirt and a playboy, which was truly evident when the army’s photographer’s assistant secretly read his hidden love letters from different ladies.

     On the course of the story, the viewers slowly learn that Goyo’s army was living in a false pretense of peace for months, and it fared well for the Americans. Villages were ravaged, and Filipinos were shot. The war seemed a lost cause for the Filipinos until they hatched a plan from the country’s geographical cornerstone—Mt. Tirad.

     It is embedded in the history of the Philippines that the said mountain is the same point where Goyo died. The greatest failure may not be the plan itself, but the betrayal of their own people. On December 2, 1899, Goyo was shot by an American soldier on Mt. Tirad in Ilocos.

     The cinematography was impeccable, and with the production—it is not a sin to say that this movie is one of the pinnacles of Filipino films when it comes to its technical prowess and aesthetic brilliance. The scoring of the movie was very fitting, and the songs have the ability to render goosebumps to the audience. The casting, which showcases veteran celebrities, was very impressive considering the movie needed hundreds of Filipino and Caucasian-looking actors to the flick. When it comes to the overall quality of the movie, its excellence was incontestable. 

     Del Pilar was not only personified in Goyo; he was humanized. Filipinos have always pictured him as the knight-in-shining-armor type of hero who bravely fought for liberty and died at the Tirad Pass. Only part of that is true, because after watching the film, the viewers witnessed the unpleasing parts of Goyo; and it left the viewers a question—is Goyo really a hero or not?

     The strongest point of the film which had the greatest intonation to the critic was how it reflected today’s very society. Goyo reflected the contemporary Philippines, and how the power dynamics is corrupted by the injustice which Filipinos like to hide in the blanket of loyalty.

     “Aso ka, hindi sundalo” was one lines of the film that everyone needs to reflect on. If Filipinos let themselves be blinded by materiality and by the sweet promises of the government, better consider themselves not human, but as dogs whose leashes are pulled by their oppressors.

     Let this movie be a reminder that Filipinos are not slaves, they are the kindred whose bloodlines hold the same very blood as those of Bonifacio, Rizal, and Del Pilar. They are Filipinos.
