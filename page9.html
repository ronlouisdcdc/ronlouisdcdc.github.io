ABSTRACT
     This phenomenological study evaluated the impact of strand shaming to the student’s strand inclination which affects their decision-making. The study included ten (10) informants, provided there are atleast three representatives from each strand who were chosen through a purposive sampling method. The researcher used three main interview questions to guide the data collection. The questions were designed to help determine the influences of strand shaming on students’ strand preference. The study’s findings revealed that strand shaming played a role in senior high school students’ strand decision-making. This paper seeks to offer an in-depth awareness of the strand shaming’s emotional effects on students that swated their personal choice of strands.

Keywords: strand shaming, decision-making, senior high students

INTRODUCTION
     K-12 Program under Republic Act (R.A.) 10533 was approved and signed by the former President Benigno S. Aquino III, on May 15, 2013. This curriculum provides two (2) additional years in High School, commonly known as the Senior High School Program. As stated by Caballero (2015), this is believed to be one of the answers in th country’s issue on education system, the program offers two (2) tracks depending on the preference of the students.

     However, due to the subject differences of each strand, a discrimination rose up between strands that has a specialized subject of Mathematics and Science than those with none (Fradiquela, 2018). This is commonly known as strand shaming (hereinafter referred to as “the phenomenon”). For three (3 years) of SHS being implemented, it still played a crucial moment for the students to decide which one to pursue. 

     Bulacan State University is a university in the City of Malolos, Province of Bulacan which offers not only a Laboratory High School but also the newly enforced Senior High School Program. The academe is one of the well-known and well-respected schools in Bulacan, and is where the researchers are currently studying.

     The academic track is for high school students who want to move in college. It will prepare you from college courses. Under this track are three strands; the Humanities and Social Sciences (HUMMS) Strand can lead a student to pursue courses related to Philosophy, English, Linguistics, Literature, Filipino and Islamic, the Science, Technology, Engineering and Mathematics (STEM) Strand, which is more focused on science, technology and math subjects, gives one lot of options on what to pick for one’s college course, and lastly, the Accountancy, Business and Management (ABM) Strand concentrates on the foundational concepts in corporate operations, financial management, business management, as well as each and every factor that revolves around those central fields.

     Observing the current millennia of students under the K-12 Program, choosing a particular strand is a common problem brought about by “strand-shaming”. Based on a study conducted regarding the strand choice and career preference of Senior High School students, it is concluded that a vast number of students are affected by this particular phenomena (Ezeonu, 2016). Nowadays, strands are being made basis of a student’s capability of learning and is being used as criteria for judging intellectual capacity. With this, students are choosing a strand in favor of societal viewpoints, instead of having an independent stand based on what they really prefer and excel at. Strand shaming also limits the youth in developing individually, being forced to take mold on what is deemed by the majority as right and generally acceptable. These statements lead to the idea of individuality, that each and every student possesses a definite characteristic, and boxed way of thinking of society discourages the youth in progressing as themselves.

     The researchers’ aim in this paper is to determine if strand shaming has an immense impact in students with regards to their selection of academic tracks in the K-12 Senior High School Program in Bulacan State University Laboratory High School. To determine the consequences of strand shaming the researchers will collect data by conducting interviews towards the participants.

     The study is mainly for the general understanding of the problems experienced by the students in different academic strands in Senior High School. The students and faculty of the school should be aware if said strand shaming is widespread in the campus and if it carries an adverse effect, then an administrative intervention may be recommended.

     The overall objective of this phenomenological study is to determine the possible effects of strand shaming on the strand preference of Grade 11 students of Bulacan State University Laboratory High School. At this stage in the research, the phenomenon will be generally defined as the circumstance where the society criticizes and degrades students based on what strand they are from.

LITERATURE REVIEW
Strand Shaming
     Panoncio (2018) claimed in his article that strand shaming is a series of trash talking between Senior High School students who have chosen strands with “hardcore” Math and Science subjects against those who did not. More specifically, the “comparatively weaker” HUMSS. This battle of strands causes huge gaps, and divides the Senior High School students.
In addition to this, Paleza (2018) stated that the issue regarding the diverseness of academic strand and subject difference in senior high school brings up a stigma which many students receive after choosing their upper secondary education track. This causes pressure that leads to poor academic performance and low self-esteem.

     Furthermore, the said matter results to discrimination which should not happen for each strand that deals with different levels of difficulty. The classified “weaker” strands should not be degraded by those other strands.

     Howard Gardner (1983) proposed the Theory of Multiple Intelligences which states that there are various of forms intelligence may partake. Logical-mathematical, linguistic, musical, social, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, and personal intelligences are aspects that are inclined with different strands. Namely, STEM, ABM and HUMSS. Lastly, Fadriquela (2018) implies that every student has their own intelligence though conducted into different strands. The classification of strands is made to specialize the skills and capabilities of students, thus the wish to excel in their selected field.
Decision-Making
     Super (1990), with regards to his Self-Concept Theory of Career Development, states that the senior high school students are at the career development’s exploration stage. In this stage, people specify their vocational choices. Due to the fact that students do not have a definite career choice, they are still easily influenced by several factors such as strand shaming. In connection to this, the study of La (2009) unveiled that parental supports, school structure, gender and grade point average have affected the Vietnamese Senior high school students’ educational and career choice. Moreover, Leonard’s (2009) study on the students of South Carolina, regarding their course selection, found out that parents and teachers are influential in the course selection decision.

     Based from the Social Learning Theory by Bandura (1997), people learn by watching what other what other people or ‘role models’ do. By simply observing another ‘model’, a student’s action could be affected by another student’s action; a student’s preference could be affected by another student’s choice. Thus, if the ‘model’ joins the bandwagon of strand shaming and discrimination, the observer’s decision would be influenced. In addition, Skinner (1953) states in his Operant Conditioning theory that people behave or do (refrain from doing) things because of rewards and punishments. Therefore, some students could have been forced to take the career they planned because of fear (negative stimulant).
Influences on Decision-Making
     Alika (2012) stated that parental and peer influence are certain factors which are involved in decision-making of adolescents.

     As stated by Taylor and Harris (2014), parents and guardians play a significant role in their children's occupational aspirations and career goal development. Thus, a parent may urge a child to have a vocation similar to their own, or one that has demonstrated high earning power. For instance, a strand which is inclined to a profitable job will certainly be the choice of the parents.

     Furthermore, Cabrera & Nasa (2000) said parental encouragement and support is a factor that influences the search and selection stages of the college choice process when savings for college affect the predisposition in deciding a course.

     Peer influence is considered a factor since in adolescent stage teenagers seek a group where he/she is socially accepted. Referring to peers’ choices affects the informant’s decision. In addition, Ambong (2017) said that interest of individuals has an impact by peers in fulfilling dreams or a certain career on account of being with them.

     Also, Wentzel (1991) observed that associating with friends who serve as academic and social resources could have a direct influence on school achievement outcomes.
Emotional Effects of Discrimination
     Discrimination and prejudice in the society has been existing for a long time, hugely affected by certain factors brought about by blind-eyed social norms and different self-centered perspectives, which in turn pushes the said phenomena, strand shaming, to occur. Social discrimination towards students in the academe created a negative impact not only on the victims who are experiencing it, but also the people around them. 

     Academic institutions has been a stepping stone and a mold for the young minds of the youth in partaking knowledge and strengthening their emotional and mental capabilities. The academe is designated to guide the youth in discovering their inner talents and skills. But in universities and schools where children are supposed to be encouraged and uplifted, discrimination and bullying are one of the daily challenges a student has to experience and overcome (Bennett, 2016).  

     The judgmental eyes of the society are one of the causes of the said phenomena to occur. According to Nadine (2014), social discrimination in schools impacts student’s ability to succeed academically. Nadine found that this prejudice induces shyness and anxiousness, planting on student’s minds the feeling of being belittled and judged. This type of bullying also created a hierarchy, a barrier between the deemed intellectuals and those seen as academically challenged.  

     As said by Ross (2002), not every individual that has suffered from social bullying has overcome the trauma and escape from the thought and feeling of being discriminated. It happens most commonly in schools despite the fact that most individuals that can help them cope up with the phenomena are present there.  

     This research is a result of the emerging situation of some senior high students being degraded and treated as outcasts due to their chosen strands (Ross, 2002). Cited in Ross’ research that 15 % to 20% of the students experience bullying during their school years and 10-20% of that are often enough to consider as serious problems (2002).       

RESEARCH METHOD
Qualitative
     The qualitative research method was used in the study because it is intact with rich detailed data that can speak through the informants’ perspectives and lead to multiple contexts in line with the under study phenomenon (Anderson, 2009).

     According to Merriam (2009), qualitative can generate a more realistic view of the world and of the phenomenon which cannot be obtained in numerical data and statistics. It involves the interest in human behavior with respect to the inter-action of these people to one another in a definite environment for example the school, workplace, auction (Heath et al,2010).

     Moreover, Crossman (2019) said a qualitative research is a type of research which gathers and interprets non-numerical data. It aims to explain the significance of the data and helps understand social life from the study of a focused populace. In addition, a qualitative research emphasizes the micro-level of interaction in a typical day basis in terms of sociology, while quantitative research subject to the macro-level of phenomena.

Phenomenology
     Phenomenological approach will be used to help the participants determine whether strand shaming affected their decision making process in choosing their strand. According to Shosha (2012), phenomenology aims to provide an in-depth comprehension of the meaning of people's lived experiences and use the understanding of phenomena experienced by people themselves. The fundamental goal of this approach is to arrive at a description of the nature of the particular phenomenon, strand shaming, and how it affected one’s perception.

     Also, Creswell (2013) stated that through phenomenology, comparative meaning of a phenomenon could be gained from multiple individuals’ lived experiences. The data would determine comparisons and contrasts of each informants’ perspective of the phenomenon, which would verify the researchers’ study. Furthermore, this type of qualitative research is appropriate in determining the effects of a contemporary phenomenon to students, because according to The Phenomenology and Science of Emotions: An Introduction of Elpidorou (2014), “emotions reveal the basic structures of human experiences”.

     Specifically, the researchers applied the logical transcendental phenomenology in collecting the data. Moustakas (1994) offered systematic procedures for using transcendental phenomenology, which engaged rookie researchers (Thompson, 2018). He remarked the difficulty and necessity of the process in order that the phenomenon could be seen in a different point of view. The process is systematic throughout, but the mind sometimes drifts. Hence, each thought set aside must be recalled again through acknowledgement, followed by the intended act (Moustakas, 1994)

     Moustakas (1994) stated that transcendental phenomenology is a “a scientific study of the appearance of things, of phenomena just as we see them and as they appear to us in consciousness”. Thompson (2018) claimed that transcendental phenomenology was applicable for a research for it removed the emphasis off the researchers, and giving it to the contemplation of the informants. The researcher’ perspectives were dismissed by concentrating on the participants’ experiences and standpoints.

Participants
     The population of interest are specific Grade 11 students from the Senior High School program of Bulacan State University Laboratory High School who experienced strand shaming. The study is aimed to gather pieces of information regarding how the issue ‘strand shaming’ affects their decision upon choosing the strand to pursue. The population was selected for the research because they are the ones who face strand-decision making and who experienced whether they have chosen the appropriate track or not. Given the population was only limited to Grade 11 students, it was a suitable group for the research topic.

     According to the Senior High School coordinator, at the time of the study, the Senior High School program in Bulacan State University had 248 Grade 11 students from three strands offered in the university. Of the 248 Grade 11 students, 46 students come from HUMSS, 55 students in ABM, and 147 students for STEM. The participants in the study were obtained from each strand, making a total of ten participants gathered from the whole population. This classification ensured that all participants had knowledge in the phenomenon to give a valid account of their educational experience.

     The number of participants in the study belong to the recommended range given by the mentor in charge in the research subject. According to Englander (2012), the researcher must be certain that the participants are capable to answer the research questions based on their experience. As stated by Marshall et al. (2013) sample sizes are not justified in qualitative research. In addition, Creswell (2013) states that 5-25 participants are the typical approach for qualitative studies. Moreover, Mason (2010) specified that the common samples used are multiples of ten.

     Prior to the interview conducted, the participants were provided consent forms that
elaborate the purpose of the study and is required to be signed. The study used interviews for the gathering of data. The interview questions satisfy the research question of the study attributal to the logical and structured manner. All the data gathered by the researchers were through face-to-face or conversational interviews. The data were later to be transcribed.

     The participants responded open-ended, communicative questions regarding strand shaming and decision-making and the influences of decision making that is experienced by the students. The researchers checked the structure and the appropriateness of the words for the interview questions. The interview questions were developed with the intent to acquire in-depth information to satisfy and answer the major research question and the supporting questions. The exact data collection procedure consisted of
     • Obtaining approval to conduct the study;
     • Providing all consent forms to potential participants;
     • Scheduling face-to-face conversational interviews;
     • Conducting interviews;
     • Transcribing interviews; and
     • Analyzing data collected.

     The researchers expected the interview questions to be answered in a reasonable period of time. The agreed amount of time for each interview was five to ten minutes. The amount of time set is sufficient, for it would guarantee the researcher and participant a relaxed and unhurried interview to obtain the necessary information. In conclusion, the researchers also took notes to apprehend the body language and voice modulation of each participant on the contrary to recording all the data in the interview.

Interview Questions
     The study conducted involved a total of 10 participants, wherein there are at least three representing students per strand. Each interviewee was asked three main questions in order to extract information, with each main question followed by supporting queries to further deepen the discussion. The interview began with a preliminary talk which includes a description of the study conducted, as well as a preview of the aforementioned phenomena, strand-shaming. This approach hopefully aims to connect with the participant better and for them to sincerely answer each of the questions with their own personal experiences. The following questions were asked to guide the interview:

     Major Question 1
          Describe strand-shaming.
     Supportive Questions to 1:
          • Are you familiar with the term strand-shaming?
          • What do you understand by the term strand-shaming?
          • Did you experience strand-shaming?
          • How did you feel when somebody “strand-shamed” you? How did you react?

     Major Question 2
          What factors lead to strand-shaming?
     Supportive Questions to 2:
          • What is your strand? Is your strand a personal choice?
          • Are you proud that you belong to this strand?
          • Do you feel inferior because of the strand you have chosen?
          • Do you think your strand is not as “prestigious” as the other strands? Why?
          • Do you think some people look down on you because of the strand you have chosen?
          • What reactions do you get when you tell people what your strand is?

     Major Question 3
          Based on your own experiences, how did strand shaming affect you and your preference of track in Senior High School?
     Supportive Questions to 3:
          • Do you think strand-shaming is a factor in your choice of strand? Why?
          • If given the chance, would you choose to follow the same strand?

DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS
     The study of the strand shaming’s influence to the decision-making of students was a substantial factor to their conclusive and preferred choices, and thus proved a definite deal of students were significantly affected by the phenomena. Though there are limited researches to sustain the details and purpose of the study, numerous articles interfaced about strand shaming and disclose facts that it is an extensive factor towards students. 

     According to the response of one participant, strand shaming is the biased and unbalanced treatment of people to the students regardless of the uniqueness of capabilities. This is the negligent treatment to what a student can contribute by means of degrading the potential credibility of the students in pursuing their specialization. Ninety percent of them imparted relative experience and background knowledge about being strand shamed.

     The results of the study revealed that strand shaming did affect 30% of participants’ population in choosing their choice of strand. The findings also proved that strand shaming is a serious phenomenon emotionally affecting Senior High School students, by means of the majority’s connotation to specific strands. The negative opinions expressed by people surrounding the participants caused negative feelings and became a factor leading to strand shaming. Hence, the whole participant-population remained proud and dignified with their chosen strands.

     Strand shaming affected the decision making of students influencing them to be indecisive towards their strand-preference. The phenomena created an impact on student’s mindset giving them a doubtful thought in transferring to a more superior strand in order to feel equal. One out of the three participants who experienced sticked to her personal preference while the other two were immensely affected by the said strand shaming therefore, they changed strands. Moreover, seventy percent of the population stand firm to their strand choice.

IMPLICATIONS 
     It has been 3 years since the Senior High School program was established, and its benefits are still in test. Despite efforts on the part of administrators to provide globally competitive curriculums and innovative learning experiences, the growing discrimination amongst strands continue to be of concern. Evidence collected from related literatures suggests that the challenge faced by the senior high school students, particularly HUMSS, are well-expressed, but not enough to provide a concrete basis, and thus, be taken not seriously. 

     The Philippines has the highest rate of economic and social inequality in Southeast Asia. This problem is not limited to personal wealth. Land distribution, basic welfare programs, educational and vocational opportunities are also affected by it (ASEAN Trade Union Council, 2011). This is the first study to explore the role of strand racism and influence in senior high students’ strand preference and choice. The research method allowed the participants of the study to provide understanding through their experiences, i.e., the strand discrimination and maltreatment experienced before, during and after choosing their strand of choice. The findings of the study complemented the vast number of existing literature supporting the concept of strand discrimination.

    Reproducing the study with a larger sample of participants from varying universities could provide an opportunity for the generalization of the study findings. The results act as major resources which helps students understand the seriousness of strand shaming and avoid committing it. The participants’ stories all implied that the current resources can significantly affect the authenticity of their strand choice. A future study involving several students from all parts of the Philippines would determine if strand shaming is a bigger problem based on the researchers’ region.
